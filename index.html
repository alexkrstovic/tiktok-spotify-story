How much does TikTok influence Spotify success?

What goes viral often gets streamed-but not always

Big Picture

TikTok and Spotify are connected - but it's complicated

Four thousand songs, four thousand stories. Each dot represents a track: the further right it sits, the more it blew up on TikTok. The higher it sits, the more it was streamed on Spotify.

If TikTok buzz always led to Spotify success, these dots would form a straight, predictable line. Instead, there is only a vast, chaotic cloud.

The data shows two different worlds. On one side are the streaming giants who don't need a viral video to stay on top. On the other are viral sensations that dominated TikTok, only to be ignored on Spotify. TikTok can spark interest, but it cannot force a hit. Research confirms a meaningful relationship between TikTok activity and streaming performance, but the connection is uneven, concentrated in a small share of songs.

Discovering the Trends

A clear pattern - with one strange twist

All 4,000 songs are grouped into five tiers by how many times they were posted to TikTok. Low means up to 100,000 posts. Medium is 100K to 1 million. High is 1M to 10 million. Viral is anything beyond that.

As TikTok activity rises, so do average Spotify streams, right up until the Viral tier. Songs with over 10 million TikTok posts actually average fewer streams than the High tier below them. Maximum exposure does not equal maximum success.

The Nostalgia Effect

TikTok doesn't just make new hits. It revives old ones.

Each dot is a song with at least 500 million TikTok views. Green dots were released before TikTok existed. The two biggest of all - "Monkeys Spinning Monkeys" (2011) and "Love You So" (2005) - have a combined 448 billion views between them.

TikTok has a way of finding sounds from years ago and making them feel brand new. The platform doesn't just reflect what's popular - it decides what gets a second life. In 2024 alone, more than 50 catalog songs charted globally after going viral on TikTok. Researchers describe this as one of the platform's most distinctive effects on the music market.

The 10 most-streamed songs on Spotify. The bars show Spotify streams, the line shows TikTok views - each on its own scale. Where the line sits high above the bar, TikTok outpaced Spotify. Where it dips, the streams came from somewhere else.

Artists Behind the Hits

Streaming giants aren't always TikTok giants

The biggest names in streaming have almost no TikTok footprint to show for it.

Each row shows two bars: green for Spotify streams, blue for TikTok posts, each scaled independently. The Weeknd alone has nearly 60 billion streams. His TikTok presence barely registers on the same chart. These artists built their audiences through albums, tours, and radio. Short-form video didn't make them. It found them already famous.

Surprises & Outliers

The rule breakers

Some of the most-played sounds on TikTok never made it onto anyone's playlist. Some of the most-streamed artists on Spotify have never had a single viral moment. Both are outliers. Both tell you something the averages can't. Research shows that just 10% of songs account for 96% of TikTok activity and 76% of Spotify streams. The rest are fighting for what's left.

Billions of TikTok views. Almost no streams.

These songs were inescapable on TikTok. Yet their Spotify streams are a fraction of what that exposure should have delivered. The moment was loud. The audience didn't follow.

The Verdict

TikTok is a tool. Not a guarantee.

TikTok helps - but only up to a point. Songs with serious TikTok activity average significantly more streams than songs with none. Go further into full virality and the numbers actually drop. The moment burns out before it converts. Industry data finds an average 11% lift in on-demand streams within three days of peak TikTok activity. Real, but short-lived.

The songs and artists that dominate Spotify built their audiences through something TikTok cannot manufacture. A fanbase that keeps coming back long after the trend has moved on. Independent researchers describe TikTok as a complementary promotional channel, not the engine of success itself.
